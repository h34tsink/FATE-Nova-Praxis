# Running Scenes -- GM Guide

A scene is the fundamental unit of play in Nova Praxis. Every scene has one central obstacle. When that obstacle resolves, the scene ends. Everything else -- atmosphere, NPC behavior, zone maps, compels -- orbits that obstacle.

This guide covers how to frame scenes, what to do inside them, how to transition between them, and what kinds of obstacles drive play forward.

---

## The Three Beats of Every Scene

### 1. Frame It

Set the stage before anyone rolls dice.

- **Describe the location.** What does it look like, sound like, smell like? Paint a sensory picture. A Kimura server farm hums with climate control and smells like ozone. An apostate bazaar on Cere is loud, crowded, and lit by mismatched neon.
- **Declare Scene Aspects (1-3).** These are the immediately obvious truths about the environment. Say them aloud and write them where everyone can see.
- **Define zones** if positioning matters (even rough verbal ones).
- **State or imply the obstacle.** The players should understand what stands between them and what they want.

> **Example:** "You're in the sublevel of a decommissioned Kimura facility. Emergency lighting casts everything in amber. Two cryo pods are cycling to active -- frost clearing from the glass. A research terminal is glowing with a single header: *Project: Standard Deviation*. Scene Aspects: *Cryo Fog Clearing*, *Emergency Power Only*. Zones: Corridor, Pod Chamber, Terminal Alcove, Storage Annex."

### 2. Play It

This is the reactive phase. Your job is to be a responsive world, not a script.

- Adjudicate rolls as players engage the obstacle
- Offer compels when Aspects create interesting complications
- Respond to player creativity -- if they try something unexpected, figure out which Skill applies and set a difficulty
- Escalate or complicate if the scene stalls
- Let players drive the action; you drive the consequences

### 3. Close It

When the obstacle is resolved (or the scene's purpose is spent), **cut**. Don't let scenes linger.

- Announce what changed as a result of the scene
- Note any Consequences players are carrying forward
- Remind the table that stress clears between scenes
- Transition to the next interesting decision

## What You Do In Every Scene

| Task | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Declare Scene Aspects** | 1-3 obvious ones up front. Hidden Aspects require Perception to notice. |
| **Define Zones** | Only needed if positioning matters. Verbal is fine -- you don't need a map. |
| **Set the Obstacle** | Physical, social, investigative, moral, or time pressure. If there's no obstacle, there's no scene. |
| **Compel** | Hit players with compels when their Aspects create drama. This is where Fate Points flow. |
| **Set Difficulties** | Diff 1-2 routine, 3 trained, 4-5 expert, 6+ extraordinary. If success isn't in question, don't roll. |
| **Refresh Your FP Pool** | 1 FP per mook in the scene + 3 FP per named NPC. Refreshes every scene. |
| **React, Don't Direct** | You set the situation. Players and dice determine outcomes. |

---

## What You Don't Do

- **Don't pre-script outcomes.** You set the situation; the fiction resolves it.
- **Don't call for rolls on trivial actions.** If failure isn't interesting, just say yes.
- **Don't block progress on failed rolls.** Failure = complication, never dead end. They get through the door -- but trip the alarm. The Savant cracks the encryption -- but leaves a trace in the mesh.
- **Don't hoard compels.** If you're not compelling, you're leaving the best part of FATE on the table. Players *want* Fate Points. Compels make their Aspects matter.
- **Don't over-describe in combat.** Quick zone layout, obvious Aspects, go. Players fill in details through their actions.
- **Don't run empty scenes.** If a scene is just "you travel to the station" -- skip it or fold it into the arrival.
- **Don't let scenes drag.** If the obstacle is resolved, cut. If the obstacle has stalled, escalate or let a player Declaration move things forward.

## Obstacle Types

Every scene needs one central obstacle. Most good scenes layer two types together. The obstacle is what makes the scene a scene -- if you can't name it, you have a transition, not a scene.

### Physical -- Something in the Way

The crew needs to get past, through, or around a tangible barrier.

| Example | Possible Skills | Diff Range |
|---------|----------------|------------|
| Biometric lock on a Kimura facility door | S. Engineering, Demolitions | 3-4 |
| Collapsed corridor after a hull breach | Athletics, Demolitions | 2-3 |
| Vacuum exposure during EVA between hab modules | Endurance, Pilot | 3-5 |
| Zero-G transit through a debris field | Athletics, Pilot | 3-4 |
| A pressure door sealing during lockdown | S. Engineering, Athletics | 3 |
| Navigating Cere's undercity without a guide | Perception, Networking (Apostate) | 2-3 |

**Tips:**
- Physical obstacles pair well with time pressure. The door is sealed *and* security is 90 seconds out.
- Use failure as complication: they breach the wall, but the structural damage creates a new Scene Aspect -- *Unstable Bulkhead*.
- Let multiple approaches work. The hacker cracks the lock (S. Engineering), the bruiser forces the door (Athletics + Demolitions), the scout finds a maintenance vent (Perception). All valid.

### Combat -- Something Trying to Hurt You

Someone or something is actively hostile.

| Example | Key NPCs | Scene Aspects |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| Ambush by Kimura corporate security in a server farm | 3 operatives + 1 Savant handler | *Server Racks Provide Cover*, *Fire Suppression System Active* |
| Lonestar drone activated in a restricted zone | 1-2 LS-7 drones | *Automated Threat Response*, *Narrow Corridor* |
| Centurion patrol intercepts the crew at a checkpoint | 2 Centurions + 1 Lonestar | *Checkpoint Chokepoint*, *Civilian Bystanders* |
| Remnant posthuman creatures in a derelict ship | 3-5 creatures | *Zero-G Environment*, *Failing Life Support*, *Darkness Beyond the Emergency Lights* |
| A Savant duel through the mesh | 1 named Savant NPC | *Mesh-Dense Environment*, *ARIS Strips Everywhere* |
| Bar fight in an apostate cantina | 4-6 minor mooks | *Overturned Tables*, *Broken Glass Everywhere*, *Someone Called the Centurions* |

**Tips:**
- Encourage Maneuvers before attacks. A round spent placing *Flanked and Exposed* makes the next attack more dramatic than two rounds of raw Firearms rolls.
- Use static dice for mooks (assume +0 on all rolls). With 7 players, this is essential for pacing.
- Name NPC intent: "The Savant is trying to black out Grace's drone feed" beats "she rolls SINC +4."
- Not every combat needs to be lethal. Corporate security might want to detain, not kill. Centurions follow use-of-force protocols. Mooks surrender when the fight turns.
- Remind players about Concession before anyone is Taken Out. Conceding lets them narrate their exit on their terms and earn FP.

### Social -- Someone Who Needs Convincing

A person or group stands between the crew and their objective, and violence isn't the answer (or shouldn't be).

| Example | Possible Skills | Opposition |
|---------|----------------|------------|
| Negotiating payment with [[Characters/Named NPCs/Briar Blackwood\|Briar Blackwood]] | Diplomacy, Guile, Networking | Insight, Guile |
| Talking past a Centurion checkpoint with a false CID | Guile, Diplomacy | Insight, Perception |
| Convincing a terrified witness to share what they saw | Diplomacy, Insight | Resolve, Guile |
| Calming a panicked NPC during a crisis | Diplomacy, Insight | Resolve |
| Playing corporate politics at a House function | Networking (House), Diplomacy, Guile | Insight, Networking |
| Interrogating a captured operative | Intimidation, Insight | Resolve, Guile |
| Talking down a standoff before it becomes a firefight | Diplomacy, Intimidation | Resolve |

**Tips:**
- Social scenes work best when both sides want something. The Centurion wants to do his job correctly. The fixer wants the best deal. The witness wants to stay alive. Find the NPC's motivation and let the players work with or against it.
- Social scenes can escalate into combat. A failed Intimidation roll at a checkpoint might trigger a lockdown. Keep combat stats handy for social NPCs.
- Compels shine here. A player's *Celebrity Status At Any Cost* Aspect might make them prioritize being impressive over being discreet.
- Social conflicts use the same stress/consequence structure as physical conflicts, but target Mental Stress. A devastating social attack can leave someone with a Consequence like *Publicly Humiliated* or *Shaken Confidence*.

### Investigative -- Something That Needs Understanding

The obstacle is knowledge. The crew needs to learn, decode, analyze, or piece together information before they can act.

| Example | Possible Skills | Mechanic |
|---------|----------------|----------|
| Decrypting data from a Kimura terminal | S. Engineering, Research | Test (Diff 3-4) |
| Analyzing a crime scene on a hab station | Perception, Insight, Research | Assessment (reveal hidden Aspects) |
| Tracking someone through mesh records | SINC, S. Engineering, Networking | Test or Contest |
| Interrogating a contact who buries truth in half-truths | Insight vs. Guile | Contest |
| Piecing together corrupted mnemonic fragments | Education, S. Engineering | Extended Test |
| Identifying an unknown Savant program signature | SINC, Research | Assessment |
| Scouring Extranet archives for a pre-Singularity reference | Research, Education | Test (Diff 2-3) |

**Tips:**
- Never gate critical information behind a single roll. If they need to know it to advance the story, they find it -- the roll determines *how much* they learn or *what it costs*. Fail by 1? They get the intel but trip a silent alarm. Fail by 3? They get a partial answer that raises more questions.
- Assessments are your best friend here. A successful Assessment reveals a hidden Aspect the player can invoke later. This makes investigation feel mechanically rewarding, not just narrative.
- Layer investigation with time pressure. They *can* decrypt the full database -- but Kimura's reclamation team is 6 hours out. How thorough do they want to be?
- Let different Skills reveal different facets. Perception spots the physical evidence. Insight reads the emotional context. Research provides historical context. SINC reads the digital traces.

### Moral/Decision -- The Obstacle Is the Choice

No roll solves it. The crew faces a decision with no clean answer, and the scene exists to make them sit with it.

| Example | What Makes It Hard |
|---------|--------------------|
| [[GM AI/Entity Cards/R4/Valare Integrated (R4 Personal Agent Ally)\|Valare's]] fork doesn't want to merge. Do you force it? | Her autonomy vs. your mission. Merging means she relives her death. |
| The data you recovered implicates an ally. Do you share it? | Loyalty vs. truth. The ally has helped you before. |
| You can escape clean or save the stranger -- not both. | Self-preservation vs. conscience. The stranger might not even be worth saving. |
| A House offers the crew a contract that would betray apostate contacts. | Money and Rep vs. relationships and principles. |
| A mnemonic core contains a person's mind -- and the client wants it destroyed. | Following orders vs. what amounts to murder. |
| The Savant on your crew can crack the system, but it means exposing their illegal programs. | Mission success vs. personal risk for a teammate. |

**Tips:**
- Don't rush these scenes. Let players talk it out in character. The tension *is* the scene.
- Compels are devastating here. Compelling someone's *Loyalty Above All Else* when the group is split on a moral question creates instant drama.
- No right answer. If one option is clearly correct, it's not a moral obstacle -- it's just a choice. Both paths should cost something.
- These scenes often work best *after* a high-action scene. The crew just fought their way out and now they have to decide what to do with what they found.
- You don't need dice for these. The obstacle resolves when the crew commits to a course of action.

### Resource/Time Pressure -- The Clock Is the Obstacle

The crew has what they need, but not enough time, resources, or margin for error.

| Example | Pressure Source |
|---------|----------------|
| Kimura reclamation team arrives in 72 hours | External deadline |
| A character's Severe Consequence is worsening -- needs medical treatment | Personal stakes |
| Faction heat is rising -- one more visible move draws a response | Escalating threat |
| The sleeve is degrading -- find a new one before the next job | Resource scarcity |
| A mnemonic core's data is corrupting -- extract what you can before it's gone | Decaying asset |
| The false CID has been flagged -- it'll be burned within hours | Closing window |

**Tips:**
- Time pressure transforms other obstacle types. A social negotiation becomes urgent when you only have one shot before the contact disappears. An investigation becomes tense when every hour spent decrypting is an hour closer to the enemy arriving.
- Make the clock visible. Tell them: "You have three scenes before the reclamation team arrives." Let them prioritize.
- Time pressure naturally forces moral decisions. "We could do this right, but we don't have time. Do we cut corners?"

## Layering Obstacles

The best scenes combine two obstacle types. This creates depth without complexity.

| Primary Obstacle | Secondary Layer | Example |
|-----------------|-----------------|---------|
| Investigative | Time Pressure | Decrypting a Kimura terminal while the lockdown clock ticks |
| Combat | Moral | Fighting through security, but one of the guards is a kid in over his head |
| Social | Investigative | Negotiating with a fixer who has the intel you need, but won't give it freely |
| Physical | Combat | Breaching a sealed corridor while drones sweep toward your position |
| Moral | Social | Deciding whether to hand over a prisoner, while the prisoner makes their case |
| Time Pressure | Physical | Evacuating through a station that's losing atmosphere, section by section |

**Rule of thumb:** One obstacle is the *what*. The second is the *why it's hard*.

## Transitions

Transitions are the cuts between scenes. They should be fast and purposeful.

### Types of Transitions

**Hard Cut** -- Jump straight to the next obstacle.
> "Forty minutes later. You're standing outside the facility's sublevel access. It's dark. The air tastes recycled. What do you do?"

**Montage** -- Skip logistics unless they're interesting.
> "You spend the next two days prepping. Grift sources a false CID. Grace calibrates her drones. Azaria calls in a favor with a Silva contact. You're ready. The shuttle touches down on Cere's dark side at 0300 local."

**Consequence Carry** -- Remind players what lingers.
> "Your arm is still torn up from that drone. The Moderate Consequence *Shredded Bicep* is still on your sheet. Stress cleared, but that didn't. What are you doing about it before the next job?"

**Revelation Bridge** -- End one scene on a question, start the next with the answer (or a deeper question).
> Scene 2 ends: "The drone's feed shows a biosignal in the sublevel. Something is alive down there."
> Scene 3 opens: "The sublevel is a sealed cryo archive. Two pods are active."

**Compel Transition** -- A character's Aspect creates the next scene.
> "Grift, your *Curiosity Kills More Than Cats* -- there's a sealed lab on the way to the exit. You passed it earlier. You're thinking about it. Are you stopping?" If he accepts the compel, the next scene writes itself.

### Transition Checklist

Before you start the next scene, handle:

- [ ] Stress clears (remind the table)
- [ ] Consequences persist (name them aloud)
- [ ] Refresh your GM Fate Point pool (count mooks + named NPCs in the new scene)
- [ ] State the new location and time
- [ ] Frame the new obstacle

### The Transition Test

Ask yourself: **"What's the next interesting decision they have to make?"**

If you can't answer that, you're not ready for the next scene. Either something unresolved from the last scene needs to carry forward, or you need to introduce a new situation.

If the answer is "they travel to the place" -- skip it. Cut to arrival. Travel is only a scene if something happens *during* travel (ambush, breakdown, tense conversation, a message arrives).

## Scene Aspects -- A Deeper Look

Scene Aspects are the single most powerful GM tool in FATE. They define what's true about the environment and give both you and the players mechanical hooks to grab.

### Writing Good Scene Aspects

**Double-edged.** The best Aspects help *and* hinder. *Emergency Lighting Only* helps someone sneaking but hurts someone aiming. *Crowded Marketplace* provides cover but limits movement.

**Evocative.** Use specific, vivid language. *Crowded* is weak. *Shoulder-to-Shoulder Apostate Crowd* tells a story and implies consequences.

**Actionable.** Players should immediately see how to use them. *It's a Room* does nothing. *Overturned Lab Tables* screams "take cover behind me."

### Scene Aspect Examples by Location Type

**Corporate Facility (Kimura, Silva, Tsarya)**
- *ARIS Strips Line Every Wall*
- *Climate-Controlled Silence*
- *Biometric Locks on Every Door*
- *Automated Security Grid Active*
- *Corporate Propaganda on Every Screen*
- *Sterile and Featureless Corridors*

**Apostate Settlement**
- *Jury-Rigged Life Support*
- *No Mesh Coverage Here*
- *Everyone Knows Everyone's Business*
- *Black Market Stalls in Every Corridor*
- *Distrust of Outsiders Runs Deep*
- *Flickering Lights and Exposed Wiring*

**Space Station / Hab Module**
- *Artificial Gravity at 0.8G*
- *Viewport Shows the Void*
- *Recycled Air Tastes Like Metal*
- *Hull Vibrations You Feel in Your Teeth*
- *Docking Bay Echo Chamber*

**Derelict / Abandoned Site**
- *Emergency Power Only*
- *Decades of Dust and Silence*
- *Structural Integrity Questionable*
- *Something Is Drawing Power That Shouldn't Be*
- *The Dark Is Absolute Beyond the Emergency Lights*

**Urban / Social Hub**
- *Shoulder-to-Shoulder Crowd*
- *Surveillance Drones Overhead*
- *Music Drowns Out Conversation*
- *Neon-Lit and Rain-Slicked*
- *Rep Transactions Everywhere You Look*

## Zones -- Quick Reference

Zones are abstract areas roughly the size of a room or corridor section. You don't need maps -- verbal descriptions work fine.

### Movement Rules

| Action | Cost | Notes |
|--------|------|-------|
| Move to adjacent zone | Free Action | No penalty |
| Hustle (2 zones) | Supplemental Action | -2 to other actions this turn |
| Sprint (3+ zones) | Athletics test (Diff 2) | Each Shift = 1 additional zone |

### Zone Design Tips

- **3-6 zones per scene** is the sweet spot. More than 6 gets hard to track verbally.
- **Give zones Aspects.** A zone without an Aspect is a missed opportunity. Even *Empty Corridor* tells players there's no cover.
- **Chokepoints create drama.** A narrow stairwell between two larger zones forces tactical decisions about who goes first.
- **Zones don't need to be symmetric.** A sniper on a balcony zone overlooking an open courtyard zone creates asymmetric advantage.

### Sample Zone Layouts

**Decommissioned Facility**
```
[Sublevel: Pod Chamber] <-> [Sublevel: Corridor] <-> [Stairwell] <-> [Ground: Lab Wing] <-> [Ground: Main Corridor] <-> [Surface Exit]
```

**Apostate Bazaar Confrontation**
```
[Food Stall Alley] <-> [Central Market Square] <-> [Warehouse Entrance]
                              |
                       [Rooftop Access]
```

**Space Station Docking Bay**
```
[Airlock] <-> [Cargo Hold] <-> [Control Room]
                   |
            [Maintenance Shaft] <-> [Engineering]
```

## Compels -- Your Best Tool

Compels are the engine of FATE drama. They turn character Aspects from static descriptions into active story drivers. If you're not compelling regularly, you're running FATE wrong.

### When to Compel

- When a player's Aspect would logically create trouble *right now*
- When a scene is stalling and you need to inject energy
- When you want to reward a player with FP for something they'd do anyway
- When you want to split the party's attention or priorities
- When a quiet player hasn't been in the spotlight recently

### The Compel Flow

1. Name the Aspect you're compelling
2. Describe the complication: "Your *Curiosity Kills More Than Cats* -- that sealed lab is calling to you"
3. Present the choice: "Accept and earn 1 FP, but you stop to investigate and fall behind the group. Or pay 1 FP to resist and keep moving."
4. **Either way, the table learns something about the character.**

### Compel Ideas by Aspect Type

**Disadvantage Aspects (D Slot)**
These are built for compels. Hit them at least once per session per player.

| Aspect | Compel Trigger | Complication |
|--------|---------------|--------------|
| *The Habit Behind the Composure* | High-stress moment | Hands shake, needs a moment to collect themselves -- costs an action |
| *Celebrity Status At Any Cost* | A camera or audience is present | Prioritizes image over mission, draws attention |
| *Curiosity Kills More Than Cats* | Unrelated mystery appears mid-mission | Stops to investigate, splits focus from objective |
| *Thin Skinned and Trauma-Triggered* | Someone else's fear or panic | Their own trauma surfaces, freezes or lashes out |
| *I Know a Truth the Coalition Would Kill to Bury* | New information surfaces | Stops to collect data, takes risks for knowledge |

**Expertise Aspects (E Slot)**
Expertise Aspects can be compelled when competence creates trouble.

| Aspect | Compel Trigger | Complication |
|--------|---------------|--------------|
| *Ghost in the System* | A tempting hack target appears | "You could crack this -- but it'd leave a trace" |
| *Born to Command* | Someone else is making bad decisions | Can't resist taking charge, steps on toes |
| *The Best Pilot You'll Never Hear About* | A risky flying opportunity | Overconfidence -- takes the dangerous route |

### Soft Compels

Not every compel needs to cost FP to refuse. Soft compels are free to decline but still award FP if accepted. Use these for:
- New players learning the system
- Minor complications that add color but don't change the scene
- Moments where you want to reward good roleplaying that already happened

**Persistent Aspects are never soft.** Always charge full price to decline a compel from a Persistent Aspect.

## Pacing a Session

### The Five-Scene Arc

Most sessions naturally fit a five-scene structure. This isn't a rule -- it's a rhythm.

| Scene | Function | Obstacle Type | Energy Level |
|-------|----------|---------------|-------------|
| 1. The Hook | Establish the situation | Social or Investigative | Medium |
| 2. The Approach | Preparation and first obstacle | Physical or Investigative | Rising |
| 3. The Twist | Complication changes the equation | Moral or Investigative | High |
| 4. The Escalation | Stakes increase, pressure mounts | Combat or Time Pressure | Peak |
| 5. The Resolution | Climax and fallout | Combat, Social, or Moral | Release |

Not every session hits all five. Some sessions are three intense scenes. Some are two long ones. The structure is a guideline, not a cage.

### Pacing Tips

- **Start in motion.** Open the session in the middle of something -- a briefing, an approach, a problem. Don't spend 20 minutes on "what do you do today?"
- **Vary the energy.** A combat scene followed by another combat scene is exhausting. Follow action with investigation or social interaction.
- **End sessions on questions.** The best session endings leave something unresolved. "The terminal displays *Project: Standard Deviation*. You don't know what that means. Yet."
- **With 7 players, cut aggressively.** Every scene will naturally take longer with more people at the table. Be willing to end scenes earlier than you think you should.
- **Track time loosely.** A briefing scene should be 10-15 minutes. An investigation scene 15-25 minutes. A combat scene 20-30 minutes. If a scene is running long, escalate or cut.

### The "Is This a Scene?" Test

Before framing a new scene, ask:

1. **Is there an obstacle?** If no, it's a transition -- skip or montage.
2. **Does it matter to the story?** If no, cut it.
3. **Will the players make interesting choices?** If no, summarize and move on.
4. **Is this the most interesting thing happening right now?** If no, cut to the interesting thing.

## Quick Reference Tables

### Difficulty Guide

| Diff | Meaning | Nova Praxis Example |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| 0 | Trivial -- auto-succeed | Opening an unlocked door |
| 1 | Easy | Spoofing a basic public terminal |
| 2 | Moderate | Picking a standard lock, basic mesh search |
| 3 | Tough | Cracking Kimura encryption, reading a hostile NPC |
| 4 | Hard | Hacking a secured military system, fast-talking a Centurion captain |
| 5 | Very Hard | Bypassing House-level security, performing surgery mid-combat |
| 6 | Exceedingly Difficult | Cracking Mimir-tech encryption, outflying a military pursuit ship |
| 7 | Seemingly Impossible | Only with stacked bonuses and luck |

### GM Fate Point Pool

| NPC Type | FP Contributed |
|----------|---------------|
| Minor Mook | 1 FP each |
| Major Mook | 1 FP each |
| Named NPC | 3 FP each |

Pool refreshes every scene.

### NPC Toughness Quick Reference

| NPC Type | Consequences | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Minor Mook | Mild only | Nameless guards, thugs, basic drones |
| Major Mook | Mild + Moderate | Tougher nameless NPCs, squad leaders |
| Named NPC | Full (Mild through Extreme) | Built like PCs, with Aspects, Stunts, gear |

### Action Economy

| Action Type | Cost | Examples |
|------------|------|---------|
| Simple Action | Your turn's action | Attack, Maneuver, Assessment, Sprint, Activate Program |
| Supplemental Action | -1 to primary roll | Move 2 zones, quick observation, draw weapon |
| Free Action | No cost | Move 1 zone, speak briefly, drop an item |
| Instant Action | Off-turn reaction | Defense roll (doesn't count against your actions) |

## Common Mistakes (and Fixes)

| Mistake | Why It Hurts | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Not declaring Scene Aspects | Players have nothing to invoke or interact with | Always open with 1-3 Aspects |
| Letting players attack without Maneuvering first | Combat becomes a dice-grinding slog | Prompt: "Anyone want to set something up first?" |
| Blocking progress on failed rolls | Kills momentum, frustrates players | Failure = success with a cost or complication |
| Forgetting to compel | Players run out of FP, Aspects feel decorative | Compel at least once per scene |
| Scenes without clear obstacles | Play drifts, energy drops, nobody knows what to do | If you can't name the obstacle, it's not a scene |
| Running mook dice individually | Combat takes forever with 7 players | Use static dice for mooks (assume +0) |
| Over-preparing NPC dialogue | You script a speech, players interrupt two sentences in | Prep NPC *intent*, not NPC *lines* |
| Not refreshing your FP pool | You run dry and stop compelling | Count mooks + named NPCs at the start of every scene |
