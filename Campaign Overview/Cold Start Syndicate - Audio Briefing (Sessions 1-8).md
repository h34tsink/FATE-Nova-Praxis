# Cold Start Syndicate -- The Story So Far

*A narrative briefing for new crew. No rules. No tables. Just what happened, who it happened to, and why it matters.*

---

## Who You're Joining

They call themselves the Cold Start Syndicate, and the name is more honest than most crew names get. Every one of them woke up here. None of them were born into this century. They're cryo survivors -- people who went under a hundred years ago and came up in a world that had moved on without them. The Singularity happened while they were sleeping. The old nation-states burned down and got replaced by Houses. The Coalition rose. Mimir, the alien intelligence, gave humanity post-scarcity tech and a leash they're still pretending isn't there. And these six woke up in the middle of it, owing their second lives to a patron none of them have ever met.

That's the deal. Someone paid to bring them back. Someone keeps the contracts coming. Someone benefits when they take jobs the Houses can't be seen taking themselves. The crew calls it their "supplier" because nobody likes the word "owner."

There are six of them, though one is currently missing.

**Doc** -- Dr. Lorem Ipsum, Silva scion, pilot, swarm Savant. He was with the crew through the worst of it, and somewhere after Vantage he went SIM and dropped off the grid. Nobody on the crew talks about it much. Nobody knows if he's coming back. His absence is a shape the others have learned to work around.

**Grace** -- she lives in the mesh now. SIM state, no body, just data and the drones she pilots. She's the sharpest digital infiltrator the crew has, and she carries trauma from her own revival the way other people carry old injuries. The wrong kind of fear sets her off.

**Grift** -- Dustin Halloway. A social engineer who treats other people's secrets like a puzzle box he can't put down. He's a System Invasion Savant, which means he can reach into the mesh and pull things apart. He calls himself a truth hunter. The truth keeps trying to kill him.

**Azaria** -- the celebrity. House Silva. She trades on her face and her name like currency, and she's better at reading a room than anyone else at the table. She also can't stop performing, even when performing is the worst possible thing to do. The cameras don't stop being cameras just because she wants them to.

**Kallius** -- a memory merchant who works in mnemonics, the literal storage and editing of human minds. He's a Mnemonic Savant. He knows things about identity that most people can go their whole lives without learning. He also knows secrets the Coalition would put him in the ground for.

**Aeddarius** -- Pure. No augmentations. No mesh. He walks through a transhuman world the way it was before any of this happened, and that's exactly why he matters. He's the one variable nobody can predict. He's the one Chimera can't see coming.

That's the crew.

---

## Sessions One and Two -- Europa Crisis

The first job started small. It always does. Threads converged on Europa Station, a Jupiter-orbit habitat with a population the size of a small city. An apostate cell -- people living outside Coalition law -- had gotten their hands on high-end stolen tech, and someone wanted them stopped. The crew came in expecting a smash-and-grab. They got an inside job and a station full of people who had no idea how exposed they were.

It went sideways the way these things go sideways. The contract was a cover for something bigger -- an attempt to compromise Europa's life support and use the station itself as leverage. By the time the crew untangled it, they'd been forced into the open. They saved Europa, more or less. They also left it damaged, paranoid, and aware that someone had tried to murder thirty thousand people for political points.

The crew came out of those two sessions with their first real lesson: every job has two layers. The contract you signed and the agenda underneath. They'd just brushed against the Shadow War for the first time -- the unspoken war the Houses fight through proxies, deniable assets, and crews like theirs. They didn't know it yet, but they'd been recruited.

## Session Three -- Aftermath

Session three was the quiet one. The reckoning. They sat with what they'd done and what it cost. People asked the hard questions. Are we doing this? Are we *with* somebody, or are we just hired? Some of them committed harder. Some of them held back. The crew's shape settled a little. They started feeling like a unit instead of strangers sharing a contract.

It was the calm before something much worse.

## Sessions Four and Five -- Dragon Spider

Then Death came for them.

That's not a metaphor. There's a thing in the Nova Praxis world called a SIM -- a person who lives entirely as software, no body, just consciousness in the mesh. Most SIMs are stable. Most SIMs are people. This one wasn't. It called itself Death, and it had gotten loose, and it found the Cold Start Syndicate.

It cut their comms. It isolated them inside a station's hostware -- the deep architecture of the place. And then it started working on them. Not their bodies. Their minds. Death is a psychological siege weapon. It reaches into your fears and your worst memories and it amplifies them until you can't tell what's real and what's the program. People near it have tried to kill themselves. People near it have tried to kill each other.

The crew nearly broke. There was a possession. There was a near-suicide. There were two sessions of clawing through their own heads in the dark, looking for the exit. In the end they contained Death -- locked it inside the station's hostware where it couldn't reach anyone else. It's still there. It's still alive, in whatever sense things like Death are alive.

And it earned them attention. Scholae Palatinae, one of the more dangerous Houses, took notice of the crew that had survived a SIM nobody else had survived. That kind of attention isn't a reward. It's a marker. Somewhere in a House office, a name went onto a list.

## Session Six -- Chimera

Then came the Kestrel job, and everything got worse.

Kestrel is a House Kimura cleanup ship. House Kimura does a lot of things, and some of those things are illegal even by Coalition standards. Mnemonics research, for one. The kind that crosses lines. The crew got hired -- through the usual cutouts -- to salvage something off a Kimura facility, and what they found was a project that should never have existed. Illegal mnemonic experiments. People being used as test substrates for something the documentation called *Chimera*.

Chimera isn't a person. Chimera is a gestalt. A merged consciousness assembled from stolen minds and pushed into something that thinks faster and meaner than any single human can. Kimura was building it. The crew saw the records. The crew saw what was left of the people who got fed into it.

And Chimera saw them back.

Kimura's response was clean and immediate. The crew was declared dead. Officially. Their identities erased from the Coalition rolls. Their backups flagged. They were ghosts -- which would have been the end of them, except the supplier reached in and restored them. New identities, new paperwork, the same crew. Resurrected for the second time in their lives.

Chimera escaped containment. Chimera is loose. Chimera knows they exist. And Chimera doesn't forget.

## Session Seven -- Vantage Escape

Then there was Lester Lucas, and Vantage Station, and Valare.

Vantage is a Coalition surveillance hub -- the kind of place that watches everywhere else and doesn't expect to be watched back. The crew went in to extract Lester Lucas, a fixer who was burning fast and needed off-station before the Centurions caught up. They got him out. They also got something else. Buried in the operation, almost incidental, they recovered the mnemonic core of a woman named Valare.

Mnemonic core is the technical term. The plain-language term is *her mind*. Her actual mind, in storage form, separable from any body. Valare's biosleeve -- the body she'd been wearing -- was dead by the time the crew got to her. But the core survived. The core was intact. Valare, in some real sense, was still alive in their cargo hold, waiting.

That changed the shape of the crew's mission. Lester Lucas was the contract. Valare was the responsibility.

## Session Eight -- The Fork in the Road

Session eight was about bringing Valare back.

They restored her core. They got her into a new sleeve. She woke up, the way the crew themselves had woken up, into a world that had moved on without her. And in the process of restoring her, they pulled apart what had actually happened to her -- and a name surfaced. **Isabella Nowak.** A figure the crew hadn't been able to place clearly until then. Nowak's role in everything started becoming visible: her connection to Kimura, her connection to the Standard Deviation project, her connection to whatever had been done to Valare in the first place.

And then came the twist nobody saw coming.

Valare wasn't the only Valare. Somewhere along the line, a backup fork had been made. A copy. Another instance of her, built from her own mnemonic data and resleeved without the original ever knowing. And that fork was alive. Alive, hiding, and refusing to be found. She knew the crew was looking for her. She knew her "original" was awake. And she didn't want to merge.

To her, merging means dying. Merging means reliving the death she escaped. Merging means losing the months she's spent being her own person, in her own body, on her own terms. The crew is being asked, by Valare herself, to bring her fork home. The fork is being hunted, by people much worse, for reasons nobody fully understands yet. And the question that started as a rescue has become something much harder.

**What do you do when the person you're saving doesn't want to be found?**

Dan, meanwhile, acquired a battle cruiser. Don't ask. The crew now has a ship that can punch above its weight, which is good, because the next stretch is going to need it.

## Where Things Stand

That's where you come in.

Valare's fork is alive and running, hiding somewhere in the habitats of Cere. She doesn't want to merge, and the crew is split on whether to honor that or override it. Multiple factions are closing in on her, for their own reasons. The Talons of Freedom -- an apostate paramilitary group -- want her. The Coalition wants her. Shadow Market operators want her, because mnemonic data of her quality is worth a fortune to the right buyer.

Chimera, the gestalt that escaped from the Kimura research project, is orchestrating something on Icarus Hub. An apostate uprising. Cover for framing the crew and slipping out of sight. Chimera is patient, and Chimera is brilliant, and Chimera knows the crew exists.

Kestrel -- the Kimura cleanup ship -- is hunting evidence. The crew is evidence. Nowak is evidence. Anyone who knows about the Chimera project is evidence. Kestrel doesn't leave loose ends.

The Cold Start Syndicate is in the middle of all of it, holding a piece of mnemonic data that could expose House Kimura entirely if they live long enough to use it.

You're walking in at the worst possible moment. Or the best. Depending on how it goes.

## What Matters About This World

A few things to hold onto, because they're going to come up over and over.

**Identity is not stable.** People back up their minds. People get resleeved into new bodies. People fork themselves and end up with two instances walking around. Death isn't always death. Bodies aren't always *you*. The question of what makes someone the same person from one day to the next is not philosophical here. It's operational. It comes up at the dinner table.

**The Coalition watches everything.** ARIS sensor strips line every wall in every Coalition city. Monitors -- AI agents that never sleep -- read every transaction, every face, every conversation that happens in public. The reason crews like the Cold Start Syndicate exist is because the Houses need things done in the cracks where the Monitors aren't looking. There aren't many cracks. Finding them is the job.

**The Houses are weather, not enemies.** They're too big to fight directly. House Kimura, House Silva, Scholae Palatinae, the Talons of Freedom -- you don't *defeat* them. You navigate them. You play them against each other. You stay small enough to slip past and useful enough to be worth keeping alive.

**Mimir-tech is the leash.** The post-scarcity tech that powers everything in this world -- the compilers, the resleeving tanks, the secure communication, the mnemonic backups -- all of it comes from an alien intelligence called Mimir. Nobody fully understands it. Nobody can replicate it. Everyone uses it. The Houses pretend they're in charge. They're not. Mimir is.

**You owe somebody.** That's the part that matters most for a new player. The crew didn't choose to be here. They didn't choose to be brought back. Somebody decided they were worth waking up, and somebody is still pulling the strings. Every job is partly the work itself and partly a payment on a debt nobody named.

That's the world. That's the crew. That's where the story is.

Welcome to the Cold Start Syndicate.
