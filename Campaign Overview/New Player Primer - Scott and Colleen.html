The World in Ninety Seconds

Nova Praxis is a transhuman, post-scarcity future. Humanity survived the Singularity — barely — and now lives scattered across the solar system and a handful of exoplanets. The old nations are gone. In their place, six corporate Houses run everything, with a thin democratic veneer called the Coalition papering over the cracks. Below both, Apostates live off-grid: black marketeers, rebels, refugees, idealists.

Your mind can be digitized, backed up, copied into new bodies. Money barely exists; reputation is currency. The Houses cooperate publicly and sabotage each other constantly in a secret Shadow War. Every job the crew takes has two layers: the obvious contract and the factional agenda underneath.

Your characters come from the same era and place as the existing crew — roughly our present. How you arrived in this century, and why, is part of what Session 0+ will establish together.

Transhumanity — The Three States

Every character picks one of three States. This is the biggest decision you'll make. It defines your relationship with your own body.

StateWhat It MeansFeel
PureOriginal body, never digitizedPrincipled, off-grid, unpredictable. +2 Refresh and extra physical ranks — mechanically rewarding.
SleevedDigitized mind downloaded into a new body (synthetic or biological)Most common. Upgradeable, swappable, flexible. Buy the body you want.
SIMNo body — pure software, works through drones and the meshDigital ghost. Lives in virtual spaces. Native to the Extranet.

Key terms you'll hear: Apotheosis (the procedure that digitizes a mind), Mnemonic Core (the physical backup of a digitized mind — if it dies, you're gone), Fork (a copy of a mind, now a separate person), Pure (people who never underwent Apotheosis). Identity is an operational question here, not a philosophical one.

The Game System in One Page

Nova Praxis runs on FATE. The creator handles the math, but you should know the feel.

The Core Roll

  • Roll 4 Fudge dice (each shows +1, 0, or -1)
  • Add your relevant Skill
  • Beat the difficulty. Margin = Shifts (bigger shifts = better outcome)

Aspects — The Heart of FATE

Aspects are short phrases that describe something true about your character or the scene. Like "Can't Stop Pulling at Loose Threads" or "This Hallway Is On Fire." They do two things:

  • Invoke — spend a Fate Point for +2 or a reroll when an Aspect helps you
  • Compel — the GM offers you a Fate Point to let your Aspect make your life harder

Good Aspects are double-edged: as useful when invoked as they are interesting when compelled. That tension is the whole game.

Stress & Consequences

Take a hit, you can soak it with stress (clears after the scene) or take a consequence (a negative Aspect that sticks around). Run out of both and you're Taken Out — or you can Concede before that and keep narrative control over what happens to your character.

The Ladder

Skills and difficulties run from Mediocre (0) up through Average (+1), Fair (+2), Good (+3), Great (+4), Superb (+5), and beyond. Most challenges land in the +2 to +4 range.

Building Your Character

The creator at shadowsynth.treppa.io walks you through all eight steps from the official guide. Here's the shape so you know what to expect:

1. Write Your Aspects (ABCDE)

Ambition (your drives), Belief (your values), Connection (person, place, or event that shaped you), Disadvantage (the flaw that bites), Expertise (your methods). Most people also write a High Concept up top — a one-line phrase of who you are. Write Aspects double-edged. Rough is fine.

2. Pick Your State

Pure, Sleeved, or SIM. Biggest choice. Shapes everything downstream.

3. Choose a Skill Spread

Three preset shapes: Specialist (one standout at +5), Expert (well-rounded, tops at +4), or Generalist (broad, nothing above +3). Pick whichever matches your concept.

4. Pick 3 Stunts

Stunts are character-defining abilities. Favorites: Savant (unlocks hacking with SINC), Licensed (carry restricted gear legally), Drone-Jock (cheaper, better drones), Specialty Aspect (free invoke on a specific skill use), Battlefield Command.

5. Pick Your Allegiance

House or Apostate. A House member gets item discounts (-1 Cost on that House's specialty gear) and access to their networks. An Apostate gets the free Non-Citizen stunt and strong ties to the black market, but no House discounts. This is a roleplay pivot as much as a mechanical one — which side do you want your character coming in from?

6. Calculate Rep-Rating

Starts at House Networking +2. It's your "reputation currency" — how much weight you pull with contacts, factions, and black-market brokers. You also get an immediate Bump (a one-time narrative favor) equal to your Diplomacy. The creator handles the math.

7. Buy Starting Gear

Gear is bought by Cost, not credits. Your first item caps at MAX(Assets, Rep-Rating) + 2. Each subsequent purchase is one Cost lower. Restricted items cap at Assets + 1 (or you need the Licensed stunt to go past that). Sleeved characters also pick their sleeve here — Alpha Bio (budget) through Epsilon Bio (top-tier), or a Cybersleeve if you want something harder-edged.

8. Stress Boxes

Each stress track (Physical, Mental, System) starts with 2 boxes and gains more from relevant skill ranks. The creator calculates this automatically. You'll feel it most when someone hits you.

Don't Over-Optimize

Mechanically optimal characters are fine, but the fun here is the friction — the compels, the ethical binds, the moments where your character's flaw matters. Pick Aspects that will get you into trouble. That's where the game lives.

What the Crew Doesn't Have

Great coverage for hacking, social, mnemonic, and command work. Thin in these areas:

  • Frontline physical presence. Nobody fights their way through a door well.
  • Stealth. They hack around locks, they don't slip past them.
  • Demolitions / combat engineering. Tactical options stop where hacking does.
  • Pilot. They have a battle cruiser and no one with Pilot +4 to fly it right.
  • Medical / biological expertise. Nobody on the crew patches bodies well.

These are gaps, not requirements. Play what sounds fun — some overlap with the existing crew is fine, and often makes for better table dynamics.

Character Ideas

Five sparks to riff on. Steal freely, mash them together, or toss them and build something we haven't thought of.

1. The Medic — "The Dead Don't Stay Dead On My Watch"

Combat surgeon / resleeving specialist. Fills the crew's biggest gap.

Concept: A trauma surgeon from the old world who now specializes in keeping bodies alive long enough to download someone else out of them. Field medicine, mnemonic transfer under fire, and the quiet certainty that death is a paperwork problem.

State: Sleeved (medical-grade bio sleeve)

Signature Skills: Mnemonics +4, Education +4, Insight +3, H.Engineering +3

Stunts: Licensed (medical gear), Specialty Aspect (triage under fire), Skilled +1

Sample Disadvantage: "I've Never Lost a Patient — And I Never Will" (the obsession that will cost them)

Why it fits: The crew takes damage they can't clean up cleanly. A real medic changes what kinds of jobs they can even attempt, and Mnemonics overlap with Kallius creates rich professional tension — two specialists, very different philosophies about bodies and minds.

2. The Wrecker — "I Make Buildings Forget They're Buildings"

Demolitions specialist and combat engineer. Tactical game-changer.

Concept: Controlled demolition was an art form before the Singularity — specialists who could drop a forty-story tower into its own footprint without scratching the building next door. That precision translates well to the Shadow War. They don't blow things up. They un-build them.

State: Sleeved or Pure

Signature Skills: Demolitions +4, H.Engineering +4, Perception +3, Stealth +3

Stunts: Licensed (explosives), Specialty Aspect (shaped charges), Transhumanist

Sample Disadvantage: "Every Room I Enter, I'm Already Calculating How to Bring It Down"

Why it fits: Opens entire new tactical avenues — the crew can literally change the shape of a scene. Creates beautiful moments where the solution to a problem is removing the floor. Also fits characters who think like engineers, not shooters.

3. The Pathfinder — "Old Wrecks Tell Better Stories Than New Maps"

Salvage archaeologist / derelict specialist. Unique investigator angle.

Concept: They made their name working dead stations and abandoned habitats — reading the bones of places that failed, finding what was left behind and what was taken. In a century built on cover-ups and sealed-off history, that's a rare, valuable skill. Part historian, part breaking-and-entering expert, part ghost-hunter.

State: Pure or Sleeved

Signature Skills: Research +4, Education +4, Stealth +4, Perception +3, H.Engineering +2

Stunts: Specialty Aspect (in a derelict), Skilled +1 (Research), Refreshed

Sample Disadvantage: "I Can't Leave a Door Un-Opened"

Why it fits: The crew hits ruins, cover-ups, and sealed spaces constantly — and nobody is actually good at reading them. A Pathfinder sees what the crew misses: structural tells, abandoned projects, the story the architecture is trying to hide. Strong fit for the current Chimera arc.

4. The Pure Brawler — "Everything I Need, I Brought With Me"

Old-school fighter. No augments, no sleeve, no apologies.

Concept: A fighter from the old world — boxer, MMA, bare-knuckle, something with a long lineage of hurting people honestly. They refused Apotheosis on principle. In a century of souped-up cybersleeves and pre-loaded combat reflexes, they're the one who hits back with nothing but intent, training, and a very strong jaw.

State: Pure (+2 Refresh, extra physical ranks, free Carpe Diem stunt)

Signature Skills: Melee +4, Endurance +4, Athletics +4, Intimidation +3, Insight +3

Stunts: Specialty Aspect (in a fight), Refreshed, Skilled +1

Sample Disadvantage: "They Keep Expecting Me to Upgrade"

Why it fits: The crew is badly under-armed physically. A Pure brawler brings real muscle with high Refresh, which is mechanically generous. Pairs thematically with Aeddarius (also Pure) — two very different Pures means the table gets to explore what that choice actually means from two angles.

5. The Shade — "I've Been in Every Room You'll Ever Try to Enter"

Virtuality-native infiltrator. Different flavor of digital specialist.

Concept: Someone who lived half their life in virtual reality before anyone else thought that was normal. Pro gamer, sim-ops pilot, VR architect — whatever the era called it. Now they move through virtualities like [[Locations/Virtual Realities/Stygia|Stygia]] and [[Locations/Virtual Realities/The Crossroads|The Crossroads]] as natives, running infiltrations in spaces where Grace's drone fleet can't follow.

State: SIM or Sleeved (with heavy mesh augs)

Signature Skills: SINC +4, S.Engineering +4, Guile +3, Insight +3, Stealth +3

Stunts: Savant (required for SINC), Specialty Aspect (in a virtuality), SIM-stunt if you go SIM

Sample Disadvantage: "The Real World Has Terrible Rendering"

Why it fits: Grace owns the mesh and drone side. This character owns the virtualities — which is where a lot of Nova Praxis's social power actually lives. Different skillset, different arena. Two digital specialists working different layers of the same stack creates fun collaboration, not redundancy.
